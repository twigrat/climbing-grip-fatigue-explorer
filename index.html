Key findings
Crimp burns out fastest. MDF FDS declined at −2.59 Hz per repetition — nearly 4× faster than sloper (−0.69 Hz/rep). Crimp concentrates mechanical load on the fingertip, generating pulley forces 5–6× body weight. It also accounts for 44% of all climbing finger injuries.
Grip differences emerge by mid-effort, not at the wall. Separation between crimp and sloper was most pronounced by Q2 — 25–50% of time-to-exhaustion. Switching to a less fatiguing grip is most effective before you feel pumped, not after.
Three independent measures all agree. MDF slope, force degradation, and time-to-exhaustion all point in the same ordinal direction: Crimp → Half-Crimp → Sloper in fatigue rate. Effect size: ηp² = .512 — exceptional
Half-crimp is the pragmatic middle ground. It accounts for 42% of all grip positions observed during climbing — its intermediate fatigue rate makes it the physiologically sensible default on sustained routes.
MDFMedian frequency. As the muscle tires, its electrical signal shifts to lower frequencies. A steeper MDF drop = faster peripheral fatigue accumulation inside the muscle.
sEMGSurface electromyography. Electrodes on the skin pick up the electrical activity your forearm muscles produce during each contraction. Like listening to the muscle's electrical heartbeat.
FDSFlexor digitorum superficialis — the forearm muscle that bends your fingers' middle joints. The key muscle driving grip-specific fatigue differences in this study.
70% MVCParticipants held 70% of their personal maximum grip force. Hard enough to guarantee fatigue, realistic enough to reflect actual climbing demands.
ηp² = .512Effect size — how much of the variation in fatigue rate is explained by grip type. At .512 this is very large. Cohen's benchmark for "large" is just .14. This tells us: these differences are real and worth studying at scale.
Pilot studyA small-scale study designed to estimate effect sizes and test protocols — not to prove a hypothesis. n=5 here is intentional. The effect sizes will power a confirmatory study of ~20–25 participants.
